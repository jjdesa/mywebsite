üß† Week 1: Key Terminologies & Foundations

Key Terminologies

  • COMPUTER: Used to manage patient care data, monitor quality, and evaluate outcomes. Networks are used to communicate data via the Internet and interact with patients on the web.
  • NURSING INFORMATICS (NI): Integration of nursing, information, and technology to support global health (IMIA, 1998).
  • CLINICAL INFORMATION SYSTEM (CIS): A system that creates, stores, retrieves, and uses patient records in healthcare.

Six Time Periods in Nursing Informatics

  1. PRIOR TO 1960s ‚Äì SIMPLE BEGINNINGS: Computers for business transactions; data on punch cards/paper tapes.
  2. 1960s ‚Äì ISSUES AND RESEARCH: CRT technology introduced; HIS mainly for financial transactions; nursing standards reviewed; vendors slowly entered healthcare.
  3. 1970s ‚Äì GIANT LEAP FOR NI: Nurses recognized computer value; participated in HIS design; MIS systems developed in the U.S.
  4. 1980s ‚Äì EMERGENCE OF NI: NI recognized as a specialty; mainframe systems integrated nursing functions; PCs became affordable and usable for nurses.
  5. 1990s ‚Äì AFFIRMATION OF NI: NI officially approved by ANA; increased demand for computer-based standards; tech used in education, practice, and research.
  6. POST-2000 ‚Äì RAPID GROWTH: Wireless, mobile, and open-source solutions emerged; EHR and privacy laws (e.g., HIPAA) implemented.

Four Major Nursing Areas

  • NURSING PRACTICE: EHR includes care plans, data, and standardized terms.
  • NURSING ADMINISTRATION: Policies and reports accessed digitally; systems track workload and resources.
  • NURSING EDUCATION: Online/distance learning; computer-based teaching methods.
  • NURSING RESEARCH: Software supports both qualitative and quantitative analysis; Internet enables access to global research.

Standards & Organizations

  • ANA (American Nurses Association): Developed NI scope and practice standards (2004, 2008).
  • JCAHO (Joint Commission): Emphasized accurate nursing documentation and acuity systems.
  • PNA (Philippine Nurses Association): Founded 1922; promoted nursing professionalization.
  • BON (Board of Nursing, PH): Governed by RA 9173; enforces standards and licensing.

Confidentiality & Security

  • HIPAA (1996): Ensures data privacy and secure handling of patient info.
  • HITECH Act (2009): Strengthened HIPAA; enforced patient consent and data security rules.

Significant Landmarks

  • 1961 ‚Äì HIMSS founded
  • 1965 ‚Äì First HIS used at El Camino Hospital
  • 1977 ‚Äì First NIS research conference
  • 1980 ‚Äì NI workshop at University of Akron
  • 1990 ‚Äì NI recognized by ANA
  • 1996 ‚Äì First Harriet Werley NI award
  • 2003 ‚Äì HIPAA deadline for electronic standards
  • 2004 ‚Äì National Health Info Coordinator office created

What is an EHR?

  • An Electronic Health Record is a digital version of a patient's chart.
  • It stores lifetime health information for multiple legitimate users.

Benefits of EHR

  • Easy accessibility
  • Multiple data views
  • Improved provider communication
  • Patient interaction
  • Integration with decision support tools
  • Access to knowledge bases

Barriers to EHR Implementation

  • Financial limitations
  • Technical issues
  • Time constraints
  • Psychological resistance
  • Social and legal concerns
  • Organizational change resistance

Challenges in Paper-Based Health Records

  • Illegible/incomplete notes
  • Scattered info (not in one place)
  • No protection against unauthorized access

EHR vs. Clinical Information System (CIS)

  • EHR: Repository of a person‚Äôs lifetime health info; usable by various healthcare professionals.
  • CIS: Computer-based system for collecting, storing, and using clinical data for care delivery.

Nicholas E. Davies & Awards of Excellence

  • Physician, HIMSS advocate for EHR.
  • President-elect of the American College of Physicians.
  • Member of the Institute of Medicine.
  • Believed EHR improves patient care.
  • Awards given by HIMSS to recognize excellence in EHR implementation.

Program Objectives (Davies Award)

  • Promote successful EHR implementations.
  • Share value and impact of EHR systems.
  • Provide recognition to organizations with high-impact systems.
  • Share best practices and strategies.

Four Key Criteria in Davies Award Evaluation

  1. Management: Strategy, planning, governance.
  2. Functionality: How EHR meets patient and organizational needs.
  3. Technology: Architecture and design.
  4. Value: Measurable ROI (return on investment).

Common Characteristics of Awardees

  • EHR seen as essential to strategic vision.
  • Clear goals for healthcare transformation.
  • Adequate funding and strong leadership.

üíª Week 3: Computer Fundamentals

Definition of a Computer

A computer is:

  • A machine with memory
  • An electronic device with logic functions
  • Automatic
  • Capable of manipulating data

Components of a Computer System

  • Hardware: Tangible parts (e.g., CPU, monitor, keyboard).
  • Software: Instructions/programs that tell the computer what to do.
  • Peopleware: Users or individuals operating the computer.

Classes of Computers

Type Description
Supercomputer Fastest computers; used in scientific and research settings.
Mainframe Large, powerful, reliable; used in big organizations.
Microcomputer Also called Personal Computers (PC); designed for individual use.
Handheld/PDA Small enough to fit in a pocket; portable and used on-the-go.

History of Computers

  1. First Generation (1940s‚Äì1950s): Used vacuum tubes. Examples: Colossus, ENIAC, Harvard Mark 1, UNIVAC.
  2. Second Generation: Used transistors; faster, more reliable, smaller.
  3. Third Generation: Used integrated circuits (ICs).
  4. Fourth Generation (Modern): Uses microprocessors; smaller, faster, and more efficient.

Central Processing Unit (CPU)

  • Known as the ‚Äúbrain‚Äù of the computer.
  • Executes basic operations called cycles: Fetch ‚Üí Decode ‚Üí Execute ‚Üí Store.
  • Speed measured in cycles per second (Hertz).

Data Units

  • Bit: Short for Binary Digit (0 or 1).
  • Byte: Group of 8 bits.
  • All computer data is stored as bits and bytes.

Basic Hardware Components

Component Description
Keyboard Allows text/data input via keys.
Mouse Hand-controlled device for navigation.
Light Pen Pen-shaped input device for screen interaction.
Voice Input Enables data entry through speech.
Scanner Converts printed pages into digital images.
Webcam Captures video and images.

Key Takeaways

  • Computers are automatic, electronic machines with memory and data-manipulating capabilities.
  • The CPU is essential for processing data.
  • Hardware + Software + Peopleware = Functional Computer System.
  • Computer generations show evolution in speed, size, and components used.

üíæ Week 4: Software & Programming

What is Software?

Software is a program that enables a computer to perform specific tasks. It is different from hardware (the physical parts of the computer).

Types of Computer Software

  • System Software: Provides a platform for other software.
    • BIOS (Basic Input/Output System): Starts the computer and manages data between OS and hardware.
    • Operating System (OS): Manages hardware, software, and system resources (e.g., Windows, macOS, Linux).
    • DOS (Disk Operating System): Older OS with text-based commands.
  • Application Software: Programs designed for end users.
    • Word Processor (e.g., MS Word)
    • Spreadsheet (e.g., MS Excel)
    • Database Software
    • Desktop Publishing
    • Presentation Software (e.g., MS PowerPoint)
    • Office Suites (e.g., MS Office ‚Äì a bundle of apps like Word, Excel, PowerPoint)

Common Software Used in Nursing Practice

  • Chat Rooms: Real-time messaging for group communication.
  • Social Networks: Online communities for interaction (e.g., Facebook, LinkedIn).
  • Electronic Bulletin Boards: Post and read messages at any time (asynchronous).
  • Listservs: Functions like mailing lists; posts are emailed to all members.

Categories of Programming Languages

  • Machine Language: Lowest level (1s and 0s), directly understood by the computer.
  • Symbolic Language: Uses symbols/mnemonics (e.g., ADD, SUB).
  • Assembly Language: Close to machine code, needs translation to run.
  • High-Level Languages: Closer to human language; needs to be translated (e.g., Java, Python, C++).

Common Programming Languages

  • BASIC: Beginner‚Äôs All-Purpose Symbolic Instruction Code.
  • COBOL: Common Business Oriented Language.
  • FORTRAN: Formula Translation (used in scientific computing).
  • Pascal: Educational programming language.

Computer Programming Process (5 Major Steps)

  1. Problem Definition
  2. Program Design
  3. Writing the Code
  4. Program Documentation
  5. Testing (Alpha Testing, Beta Testing)

Definition of a Computer System

A computer system is:

  • A complex entity (like the human body)
  • Made up of peripherals, computer box, and software
  • Designed to perform computing functions

System Theory Core Concepts

  • Input: Feeds from the external environment that start system functions.
  • Processing: Mathematical, logical, or data transfer operations (CPU, RAM, ROM).
  • Output: End product or result of the process.
  • Control: Prevents or corrects errors in input, process, or output.
  • Feedback: Output reused as input for system self-regulation.
  • Interdependence: System parts depend on each other and interact.

System Classifications

  • Closed System: Has clear and sealed boundaries; highly restricted input; doesn‚Äôt interact freely with environment.
  • Open System: Has fluid boundaries; integrates and interacts with other systems; may be a subsystem of a bigger system.

Information System Definition

A set of people, procedures, and resources that collects, transforms, and distributes information in an organization.

Types of Information Systems

  • Management Information System (MIS): Helps managers track business operations (e.g., financial reports, hospital occupancy).
  • Transaction Processing System (TPS): Handles repetitive operations and order updates in real time.
  • Physiologic Monitoring System (PMS): Automatically tracks vital signs and continuous physiological data.
  • Knowledge Work System (KWS): Helps skilled professionals develop and apply new knowledge.
  • Office Automation System (OAS): Manages documents and internal communications (e.g., emails, word processing).
  • Executive Support System (ESS): Assists senior managers in strategic decision-making.
  • Bibliographic Retrieval System: Stores and retrieves bibliographic or literature-based data.
  • Stand-Alone / Dedicated / Turnkey System: Designed for one specific application only.
  • Decision Support System (DSS): Assists users in decision-making, without replacing human judgment.
  • Expert System: Simulates expert reasoning to make decisions in specific scenarios.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI) System: Mimics human reasoning; can learn and adjust based on new data.
  • Natural Language System: Processes human language (voice or handwriting).

Hospital Information System (HIS)

Purpose: Manages medical and administrative hospital data; helps health professionals work effectively and efficiently.

Modules: Order Entry System, Order Entry Result Reporting Module, Charting Systems, Lab, Pharmacy, Radiology Module, POS System, Semiclinical and Clinical Modules.

Types of Networks

  • LAN (Local Area Network): Small area (e.g., office).
  • MAN (Metropolitan Area Network): Covers a city.
  • WAN (Wide Area Network): Covers large geographic areas.
  • HAN (Home Area Network): Personal home devices.
  • CAN (Campus Area Network): Used in schools or campuses.

Definitions

  • Internet: A system architecture that has revolutionized communications and methods of commerce by allowing various computer networks around the world to interconnect. Sometimes referred to as a "network of networks."
  • Mobile Computing: Human‚Äìcomputer interaction where a computer is transported during normal usage, allowing data transmission (voice, video). Involves mobile communication, hardware, and software.

History and Core Concepts of the Internet

  • Vannevar Bush: Associated with "MEMEX" system (automated library system).
  • Norbert Wiener: Invented "CYBERNETICS" (using technology to extend human capabilities).
  • Marshall McLuhan: Popularized "GLOBAL VILLAGE" interconnected by an electronic nervous system.
  • Dr. Joseph Carl Robnett Licklider: Formulated earliest ideas of global networking in 1962, envisioning an "Intergalactic Computer Network" with modern Internet features (graphical computing, user-friendly interfaces, digital libraries, e-commerce, online banking, cloud computing).
  • Paul Baran & Donald Davies: Discovered "Packet Switching."
  • Lawrence Gilman Roberts: Led network development based on packet switching.
  • Vint Cerf & Robert Elliot Kahn: Co-designed TCP/IP protocols.
  • TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol): Fundamental communication protocols.
  • Packet Switching: Underlying concept for network development.
  • Interface Message Processor (IMP): First packet-router, precursor to modern Internet routers.

Internet Characteristics

  • Worldwide network of interconnected computer networks using standard communication protocols.
  • Highly decentralized and independent of underlying physical media.
  • Traffic can travel through copper wire, coaxial cable, optical fiber, and radio waves.

üõ°Ô∏è Week 11: Dependable Nursing Information Systems

Dependable Nursing Information Systems Context

The healthcare industry is transitioning from an inefficient, costly, manually intensive, and crises-driven model of care to a more efficient, consumer-centered, science-based model focused on health management.

Dependability Definition

Ethical obligations drive requirements for system reliability, availability, confidentiality, data integrity, responsiveness, and safety, which are collectively referred to as dependability.

Dependability Measure

Dependability is a measure of the extent to which a system can justifiably be relied on to deliver its expected services.

Attributes of Dependability

  • System Reliability: The system consistently behaves the same way.
  • System Availability: The system is operational and accessible when needed.
  • Confidentiality: Protecting sensitive information from unauthorized access.
  • Data Integrity: Ensuring data is accurate, complete, and consistent.
  • Responsiveness: The system reacts to user input and performs tasks within an acceptable time frame.
  • Safety: The system does not cause harm to patients or users.

Clinical Information System (CIS)

  • Problem: Healthcare providers struggle to track a staggering amount of information, and failure to do so can detrimentally affect patient care.
  • Solution: A Clinical Information System (CIS) is designed to manage patient data in the information age.
  • Purpose: It is intended to replace the Medical Records Department of a medical institution, supporting the acquisition, storage, manipulation, and distribution of clinical information throughout the organization.

Phases of System Development/Implementation

A. The Planning Phase:

  • Initiation: Begins when an organization determines that an existing need or problem can be solved by developing or implementing a CIS or application.
  • 1. Definition of the Problem: Includes a description of how the system will be evaluated. Establishing evaluation criteria early supports the philosophy of "beginning with the end in mind". Realistic goals describe the expected results and improvements from the system.
  • 2. Data Collection: First step in system analysis, involving collection of data reflecting the existing problem or goal. Thorough data collection may lead to refinements in the project scope agreement and added benefits.
  • 3. Data Analysis: Provides data for developing an overview of the nursing problem and/or stated goal defined in the project scope agreement. Tools: Data flowchart, Grid chart, Decision table, Organizational Chart Model.
  • 4. Data Review: Focuses on resolving problems or attaining goals defined in the feasibility study based on workflow documents and functional design. Recommendations for streamlining workflow are suggested.
  • 5. Benefits Identification: Should reflect the resolution of the identified problem, formulated and stated in quantifiable terms. Proposed benefit statements become criteria for measuring the success of the problem.
  • 6. System Proposal Development: Includes submission to the project‚Äôs steering committee for review and approval. Final proposal forms the basis for the next phase.

‚ù§Ô∏è Week 13: Critical Care & Community Health

Critical Care Nurse

  • Definition: A licensed professional nurse responsible for ensuring that acutely and critically ill patients receive optimal care.
  • Practice Focus: Complex assessment, high-intensity therapies and interventions, and continuous nursing vigilance.

Clinical Care Information System (CCIS)

  • Definition: A system designed to collect, store, organize, retrieve, and manipulate all data related to the care of critically-ill clients.
  • Components: Patient Management, Vital Signs Monitoring, Diagnostic Testing Results, Clinical Documentation, Clinical Decision Support, Medication Management, Healthcare Provider Order Entry.

Information Technology Capabilities in Critical Care Settings

  • Process, store, and integrate physiologic and diagnostic information from various sources.
  • Present deviations from preset ranges by an alarm or an alert.
  • Accept and store patient care documentation in a lifetime clinical repository.
  • Trend data over time for analysis.

EMR vs. EHR

  • Electronic Medical Record (EMR): Digital version of the paper charts in the clinician‚Äôs office. Contains medical and treatment history from one practice.
  • Electronic Health Record (EHR): Designed to share information with other healthcare providers, such as laboratories and specialists. Contains a more comprehensive patient history.

Meaningful Use

Using certified EHR technology to:

  • Improve quality, safety, efficiency, and reduce health disparities.
  • Engage patients and family.
  • Improve care coordination and population and public health.
  • Maintain privacy and security of patient health information.

Community Health Network System

  • Functions: Download patient records from the hospital to the home database. Enter a set of questions about symptoms using expert system logic until pathways are concluded. Track self-care and, depending on responses, call or make an appointment with a clinician. Provide additional information on the condition if self-care is appropriate.

Future of Nursing Informatics

  • Nursing Informatics uses computer technology to decrease costs, increase patient-nurse interactions, and decrease redundancy of documentation.
  • It helps the healthcare industry stay marketable in a changing world.
  • It aids nurses by helping to obtain important data for patients and guiding decisions in patient care.
  • It is a growing field for advancement and offers potential areas for cost containment.

The Role of Informatics in Nursing

  • Informatics nurses are key people.
  • Information is power and critical for decision-making.
  • Nursing is a cognitive profession, with the core of nursing (ADPIE - Assessment, Diagnosis, Planning, Implementation, Evaluation) taking place in nurses' minds.
  • Key actions: Identify potential problems. Identify changes in patient status that can occur quickly. Ensure information is readily available. Interpret, systematize, and arrange data. Formalize an appropriate plan of action. Align Nursing Best Practice with Clinical workflows & care. Improve clinical policies, protocols, processes & procedures. Standardizing Nursing Language: The use of a template can remind nurses of important information required in patient care documentation.

Telehealth

  • Savings: Provides savings in improved access to care, allowing clients to be treated earlier and with fewer interventions. Clients receive treatment in their own community where services cost less.
  • Improves quality of care and continuity of care through convenient follow-up.
  • Applications: Vary greatly, including client monitoring, diagnostic evaluations, decision support systems, storage and retrieval, research, voice recognition for dictation, and education of healthcare professionals and consumers.
